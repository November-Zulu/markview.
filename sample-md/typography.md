# Typography

Typography is **the art and technique of arranging type** to make written language legible,
readable, and appealing when displayed. The practice encompasses selecting typefaces, point
sizes, line lengths, spacing, and kerning.

The term derives from Greek roots: *typos* ("type") and *-graphia* ("writing").

## History

### Early Origins

Typography's history traces to ancient practices using punches and dies for seals and
currency. Notable early examples include:

- **Mesopotamian brick stamps** (second millennium BC)
- The **Phaistos Disc** (1850--1600 BC)
- Medieval artifacts like the **Pruefening Abbey inscription** (1119)

### Movable Type

The invention of movable type revolutionized human communication:

1. **Bi Sheng** invented ceramic movable type during China's Song dynasty (c. 1045)
2. **Metal movable type** emerged in Korea circa 1230
3. **Wang Zhen** pioneered wooden type
4. **Johannes Gutenberg** developed lead-based movable type around 1439, sparking the
   *Printing Revolution*

### Modern Evolution

The 1960s brought camera-ready typesetting and dry transfer technology. **Digital typography**
emerged in the 1980s with personal computers and software like *Fontographer*,
democratizing type design and creation.

## Key Typeface Styles

**Roman Typefaces** --- Based on Greek lapidary letters and Roman monumental capitals. Two
sub-styles exist: *old style* (similarly weighted lines) and *modern* (contrasting light and
heavy lines).

**Blackletter / Gothic** --- Early European printing imitated hand-lettering styles but
proved difficult to read at scale.

## Three Fundamental Principles

> **Legibility** refers to distinguishing individual characters --- "the quality of being
> decipherable and recognisable."

> **Readability** concerns how easily text flows as a whole unit, influenced by margins,
> spacing, and structure.

> **Aesthetics** balances visual appeal with functional communication.

## Applications

Typography appears across diverse contexts:

- Advertisements and promotional materials
- Signs, billboards, and posters
- Packaging and logos
- Architectural lettering
- Digital media and web design

## The Digital Transformation

Digital character encoding separated text *presentation* from *meaning*, allowing flexible
application across contexts. Contemporary typographers work with dynamic digital materials
including web pages and screen displays, expanding far beyond traditional print media.
