# Chess

Chess is one of the oldest and most enduring strategy games in the world, played by millions of people across every continent. Its blend of tactical calculation, strategic planning, and creative expression has captivated minds for over a thousand years.

## Origins and History

Chess emerged from **chaturanga**, an ancient Indian game from the 7th century. The game spread through Persia and the Arab world before reaching Europe. The modern rules -- including the powerful queen and long-range bishop -- developed in Europe by the late 15th century and became standardised internationally by the 19th century.

## Game Fundamentals

### Board and Pieces

Chess is played on an **8x8 board** with 64 alternating light and dark squares. Each player commands 16 pieces:

- 1 **King**
- 1 **Queen**
- 2 **Rooks**
- 2 **Bishops**
- 2 **Knights**
- 8 **Pawns**

*White always moves first.*

### Piece Movements

| Piece | Movement |
|-------|----------|
| King | One square in any direction |
| Queen | Any distance along ranks, files, or diagonals |
| Rook | Any distance along ranks or files |
| Bishop | Any distance diagonally |
| Knight | L-shape (two squares + one square perpendicular) |
| Pawn | Forward one square (or two on first move); captures diagonally |

### Special Moves

1. **Castling** -- The king moves two squares toward a rook, and the rook jumps to the square the king crossed. Requires that neither piece has moved, no pieces stand between them, and the king is not in check.
2. **En passant** -- A pawn may capture an opponent's pawn that has just advanced two squares, moving diagonally behind it.
3. **Promotion** -- When a pawn reaches the opposite end of the board, it transforms into a queen, rook, bishop, or knight (most commonly a queen).

## Winning and Drawing

A player wins by achieving **checkmate** -- placing the opponent's king under inescapable attack. Games can also end through resignation, timeout, or forfeit.

Draws result from:

- **Stalemate** -- no legal moves available for the player to move
- **Dead position** -- checkmate is impossible with remaining pieces
- **Mutual agreement** between players
- **Threefold repetition** of the same position
- **Fifty-move rule** -- 50 moves without a capture or pawn advance

## Strategic Concepts

> Chess strategy involves evaluating positions through multiple lenses, balancing short-term tactics with long-term planning.

### Positional Evaluation

- **Material count**: Pawns = 1 point; knights and bishops ~ 3; rooks = 5; queens = 9
- **Pawn structure**: The configuration of pawns largely determines strategic possibilities
- **King safety**: Protecting one's king while exploiting the opponent's vulnerabilities
- **Centre control**: Dominating the central squares provides tactical and spatial advantages

### Tactics

Tactics are short-term manoeuvres that exploit specific features of a position:

- *Pins* -- a piece cannot move without exposing a more valuable piece behind it
- *Forks* -- one piece attacks two or more enemy pieces simultaneously
- *Skewers* -- an attack on a valuable piece that, when moved, exposes a piece behind it
- *Discovered attacks* -- moving one piece reveals an attack by another
- *Deflections* -- forcing an enemy piece away from a key defensive square

## Notable Players and World Champions

**Wilhelm Steinitz** became the first universally recognised World Chess Champion in 1886. Throughout the 20th and 21st centuries, legendary players have shaped the game:

- **Jose Raul Capablanca** -- dominated the 1920s-1930s with effortless positional mastery
- **Bobby Fischer** -- revolutionised chess in the 1960s-1970s; his 1972 World Championship match against Spassky captivated the world
- **Garry Kasparov** -- ruled from 1985-2000, achieving unprecedented ratings and becoming chess's most famous ambassador
- **Magnus Carlsen** -- dominated the modern era since 2013, widely regarded as one of the greatest players ever
- **Gukesh Dommaraju** -- current World Champion, having won the title in 2024

## FIDE and Competitive Chess

The **International Chess Federation** (*Federation Internationale des Echecs*, or FIDE) governs competitive chess worldwide. Founded in 1924, FIDE is responsible for:

1. Establishing and maintaining the official rules of chess
2. Organising the World Championship cycle and Olympiad
3. Maintaining the **Elo rating system** for ranking players
4. Awarding titles: *Grandmaster* (GM), *International Master* (IM), *FIDE Master* (FM)

## Computer Chess and Artificial Intelligence

Chess has long served as a benchmark for artificial intelligence research.

- **1997** -- IBM's *Deep Blue* defeated reigning World Champion Garry Kasparov, a watershed moment demonstrating computational chess superiority
- **2017** -- Google DeepMind's *AlphaZero* learned chess from scratch through self-play, developing a creative and unconventional style that stunned the chess world

Modern chess engines far surpass human capabilities and have fundamentally altered **opening theory** and **endgame analysis**, influencing how professional players train and prepare for competition.

## Online Chess and Modern Expansion

Digital platforms like [Chess.com](https://www.chess.com) and [Lichess](https://lichess.org) have democratised chess access, enabling players worldwide to compete in real-time. Time controls range from *classical* (hours per side) to *bullet* (under three minutes total), and online streaming has created a new generation of chess content creators and fans.

## Chess in Culture

Chess permeates Western culture through literature, film, and visual arts. The game connects meaningfully to mathematics, computer science, and psychology. Its complexity -- described as *"not a solved game"* -- ensures continued intellectual engagement across centuries.
