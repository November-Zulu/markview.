# Coffee

Coffee is a **darkly colored, bitter, and slightly acidic** beverage with a stimulating effect
on humans, primarily due to its caffeine content. It is one of the most popular drinks in the
world and the backbone of a global industry worth **$495.50 billion** as of 2023.

## Etymology

The word "coffee" entered English in 1582 via Dutch *koffie*, borrowed from Ottoman Turkish
*kahve*, which came from Arabic *qahwah*. The Arabic term likely meant "the dark one,"
referring to the brew's color.

## History

### Early Origins

> "The earliest credible reports of coffee drinking pertain to the plant's use among the
> Sufis of Yemen" in the mid-15th century.

Legend attributes the discovery of coffee to a 9th-century Ethiopian goatherd named **Kaldi**,
who supposedly noticed his goats becoming energetic after eating coffee berries. However, this
story first appeared in 1671 and is likely apocryphal.

### Global Expansion

The spread of coffee followed a fascinating trajectory across the world:

- By the **late 15th century**, coffee drinking was established in Yemen
- The Ottoman Empire's first coffeehouse opened in **1555** in Istanbul
- **Pope Clement VIII** declared it a Christian beverage in 1600, enabling European adoption
- The first European coffeehouse outside Ottoman territory opened in **Venice in 1647**

### Colonial Era

The **Dutch East India Company** first imported coffee on a large scale, growing it in Java
and Ceylon. By 1711, Indonesian coffee reached the Netherlands. In the Americas, *Gabriel de
Clieu* brought coffee to Martinique in the 1720s, establishing arabica cultivation throughout
the region.

During the American Revolutionary War, coffee demand surged as dealers had to *"hoard their
scarce supplies"* to meet the growing appetite for the beverage.

## Biology and Cultivation

### Main Species

Two commercially cultivated species dominate the global market:

1. ***Coffea arabica*** --- comprises about three-quarters of global cultivation and is
   considered superior in quality
2. ***Coffea canephora*** (robusta) --- contains **40--50% more caffeine** than arabica and
   is commonly used in commercial blends

### Growing Regions

Coffee plants thrive within the **"bean belt"** --- the tropical zone between the Tropics of
Cancer and Capricorn. In 2023, the leading producers were:

| Country  | Share of Global Production |
|----------|---------------------------|
| Brazil   | 31%                       |
| Vietnam  | 2nd largest               |

## Processing

### From Berry to Bean

After harvesting, coffee berries undergo either **dry** or **wet** processing. In traditional
African methods, the pulped and fermented coffee is spread thinly on raised beds for drying.

### Specialty Varieties

Some of the world's most expensive coffees involve unusual processing methods:

- **Kopi luwak** --- Indonesian coffee processed through Asian palm civets' digestive systems,
  reaching *$160 per pound* or $30 per brewed cup
- **Thai black ivory coffee** --- fed to elephants, selling for up to *$1,100 per kilogram*

### Roasting

The roasting process begins when the temperature inside the bean reaches approximately
**200 degrees Celsius**. During roasting, caramelization transforms starches into sugars that
brown the beans. Interestingly, caffeine content differences between light and dark roasts
are only about **0.1%**.

## Preparation Methods

### Common Techniques

- **Espresso** --- forces hot pressurized water through finely-ground coffee at 9 bar pressure
- **Turkish coffee** --- ground to powder and boiled in a traditional *cezve*
- **Cold brew** --- steeps coarsely-ground beans in cold water for hours
- **Pour over** --- hot water poured slowly through a filter of ground coffee

> Optimal extraction occurs between **91 and 96 degrees Celsius**.

### Serving Styles

Coffee is served in many forms: black, with milk or cream (*white coffee*), iced, and as
espresso-based drinks like **lattes**, **cappuccinos**, and **macchiatos**.

## Economic and Environmental Impact

Despite coffee's massive economic value, **coffee farmers disproportionately live in poverty**.
The industry also faces significant environmental criticism:

- **Deforestation** from expanding coffee plantations
- **Water consumption** --- producing one kilogram of roasted coffee requires approximately
  *26,400 liters of water*
- Traditional **shade-grown** coffee provides habitat for wildlife, while modern sun
  cultivation requires increased use of fertilizer and pesticides

As of 2016, at least **34%** of global coffee production was compliant with voluntary
sustainability standards like *Fairtrade* and *UTZ certification*.

## Cultural Significance

Coffeehouses became important **social institutions** across many cultures, serving as places
for intellectual discussion, business meetings, and community gathering. The first coffeehouses
in the Ottoman Empire were known as *qahveh khaneh* and quickly became centers of social
activity, earning the nickname **"schools of the wise."**

Coffee holds deep cultural significance in societies around the world:

- In **Ethiopia**, the coffee ceremony is an important social ritual involving roasting,
  grinding, and brewing beans in front of guests --- a process that can take over an hour
- In **Italy**, espresso culture dictates strict social norms: cappuccino is a morning drink,
  never ordered after lunch
- In **Turkey**, coffee preparation and serving is a UNESCO-recognized *Intangible Cultural
  Heritage*
- In **Scandinavian countries**, *fika* (Sweden) and coffee breaks are deeply embedded in
  daily life, with Finland and Norway among the highest per-capita consumers

### Coffeehouses and Intellectual Life

> The coffeehouses of 17th-century England were called "penny universities" --- for the price
> of a penny cup of coffee, anyone could sit and listen to extraordinary conversations.

Throughout history, coffeehouses have served as incubators for revolutionary ideas, artistic
movements, and scientific discourse. The Lloyd's of London insurance market, for example,
originated in Edward Lloyd's coffeehouse in the 1680s.

## Health Effects

Research on coffee's health effects has produced a nuanced picture:

**Potential benefits:**
- Associated with reduced risk of **type 2 diabetes**, Parkinson's disease, and certain
  liver conditions
- Contains **antioxidants** and beneficial compounds beyond caffeine
- Moderate consumption (3--4 cups daily) linked to lower all-cause mortality in some studies

**Potential concerns:**
- Excessive caffeine can cause **anxiety**, insomnia, and increased heart rate
- May raise blood pressure temporarily in non-habitual drinkers
- Pregnant individuals are generally advised to limit intake to **200 mg of caffeine** per day
