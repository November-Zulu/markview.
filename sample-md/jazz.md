# Jazz

Jazz is a music genre that originated in **African-American communities** of New Orleans in
the late 19th and early 20th centuries. It is characterized by improvisation, swing rhythms,
and a rich fusion of musical traditions.

> "It's all music."
> --- *Duke Ellington*

## Origins

Jazz synthesized multiple musical traditions into something entirely new:

- **African elements** --- rhythmic patterns, call-and-response vocals, polyrhythms
- **European influences** --- harmonic structures, classical instruments
- **Caribbean rhythms** --- tresillo patterns, habanera rhythms from Cuban music
- **American forms** --- blues, ragtime, spirituals, work songs

New Orleans proved crucial to jazz development. The city's unique cultural blend --- where
enslaved and free people of color maintained African musical traditions --- created ideal
conditions. **Congo Square** hosted African-based dances and music until 1843, preserving
rhythmic traditions that slavery had suppressed elsewhere in America.

## Core Elements

**Improvisation** stands as jazz's defining characteristic. Unlike classical music's emphasis
on fidelity to a written composition, jazz performers interpret pieces individually, varying
melodies, harmonies, and time signatures based on mood and interaction with other musicians.

**Swing** is described as *"an intangible rhythmic momentum."* As **Louis Armstrong** famously
stated: "If you don't feel it, you'll never know it."

## Etymology

The term "jazz" likely derives from *"jasm,"* a slang word meaning "pep, energy" dating to
1860. The word first appeared in a musical context in a 1912 *Los Angeles Times* article
and was documented in New Orleans music by 1916.

## Key Stylistic Periods

1. **Ragtime Era** --- Scott Joplin's *"Maple Leaf Rag"* (1899) introduced syncopated rhythms
2. **Jazz Age (1920s--1930s)** --- speakeasies, King Oliver, Jelly Roll Morton
3. **Bebop (1940s)** --- faster tempos, chord-based improvisation, a "musician's music"
4. **Hard Bop** --- incorporated rhythm and blues and gospel elements
5. **Modal Jazz** --- abandoned chord progressions for greater improvisational freedom
6. **Free Jazz** --- explored playing without regular meter or formal structures
7. **Jazz Fusion** --- combined jazz improvisation with rock rhythms and electronics

## Notable Figures

- **Louis Armstrong** --- popularized New Orleans trumpet style, codified swing
- **Duke Ellington** --- one of jazz's most celebrated composers and bandleaders
- **Ella Fitzgerald** --- legendary vocalist known for her scat singing
- **Billie Holiday** --- transformed jazz vocal performance
- **Benny Goodman** --- the *"King of Swing,"* led jazz's first Carnegie Hall concert in 1938
- **Lil Hardin Armstrong** --- pianist and composer who shaped early jazz
- **Melba Liston** --- the first female horn player to work in major bands

## Cultural Impact

Jazz reflected America's racial complexities. While originating in African-American
communities, the genre became a space where racial barriers were challenged. **Papa Jack
Laine** integrated Black and white musicians in his marching band, and these integrated
ensembles helped *"change attitudes toward race in the U.S."*

During World War II, the **International Sweethearts of Rhythm** became the first integrated
all-female band to tour with the USO in Europe.
