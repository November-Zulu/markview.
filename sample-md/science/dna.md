# DNA: Deoxyribonucleic Acid

**DNA** (deoxyribonucleic acid) is the molecule that carries the genetic instructions for the development, functioning, growth, and reproduction of all known living organisms. It is often called the "blueprint of life."

## Discovery of the Double Helix

The story of DNA's structure is one of the most celebrated episodes in the history of science.

### Key Figures

- **Rosalind Franklin** and **Maurice Wilkins** produced critical X-ray crystallography images of DNA fibres. Franklin's famous *Photo 51* (1952) provided essential evidence for the helical structure.
- **James Watson** and **Francis Crick**, working at the Cavendish Laboratory in Cambridge, used Franklin's data along with model-building techniques to propose the **double helix** structure in **1953**.
- Watson, Crick, and Wilkins received the 1962 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine. Franklin, who had died in 1958, was not eligible for the award.

### Earlier Contributions

- **Friedrich Miescher** first isolated DNA (which he called "nuclein") from white blood cells in 1869.
- **Erwin Chargaff** established that the amounts of adenine and thymine are roughly equal, as are guanine and cytosine -- a key clue to the base-pairing rules.
- **Oswald Avery**, **Colin MacLeod**, and **Maclyn McCarty** demonstrated in 1944 that DNA, not protein, is the molecule of heredity.

## Structure

### The Double Helix

DNA consists of two polynucleotide chains that wind around a common axis in a right-handed helix. Key dimensions:

- **Pitch (one full turn):** 34 angstroms (3.4 nm)
- **Helix diameter:** approximately 20 angstroms (2.0 nm)
- **Distance between base pairs:** 3.4 angstroms

The two strands run in *antiparallel* directions (one 5' to 3', the other 3' to 5') and are held together by hydrogen bonds between complementary bases.

### Nucleotide Building Blocks

Each nucleotide consists of three components:

1. **A nitrogenous base** (one of four types)
2. **A deoxyribose sugar** (five-carbon ring)
3. **A phosphate group**

Nucleotides are linked by **phosphodiester bonds** between the sugar of one nucleotide and the phosphate of the next, forming the sugar-phosphate backbone.

## The Four Bases and Base Pairing

DNA uses four nitrogenous bases, divided into two chemical families:

**Purines** (two-ring structure):
- **Adenine (A)**
- **Guanine (G)**

**Pyrimidines** (one-ring structure):
- **Thymine (T)**
- **Cytosine (C)**

### Complementary Base Pair Table

| Base | Pairs With | Bond Type | Number of H-Bonds | Chemical Family |
|------|-----------|-----------|-------------------|----------------|
| **Adenine (A)** | Thymine (T) | Hydrogen bonds | 2 | Purine |
| **Thymine (T)** | Adenine (A) | Hydrogen bonds | 2 | Pyrimidine |
| **Guanine (G)** | Cytosine (C) | Hydrogen bonds | 3 | Purine |
| **Cytosine (C)** | Guanine (G) | Hydrogen bonds | 3 | Pyrimidine |

> A purine always pairs with a pyrimidine, keeping the helix width constant. DNA regions with high **GC content** are more thermally stable because G-C pairs have three hydrogen bonds compared to two for A-T pairs.

## DNA in the Cell

### Chromosomes

In **eukaryotic** cells, DNA is packaged into structures called chromosomes within the nucleus. The human genome contains approximately **3 billion base pairs** distributed across **46 chromosomes** (23 pairs).

- The largest human chromosome (chromosome 1) has ~220 million base pairs.
- If fully stretched, the DNA in a single human cell would extend about **2 metres**.

### DNA Packaging

To fit inside the nucleus, DNA undergoes extensive compaction:

1. DNA wraps around **histone** protein octamers, forming structures called *nucleosomes*
2. Nucleosomes coil into a **30 nm fibre**
3. Further folding produces increasingly condensed chromatin
4. During cell division, chromatin condenses maximally into visible chromosomes

### Other Locations

DNA is not found only in the nucleus:

- **Mitochondrial DNA (mtDNA)** -- small circular genome in mitochondria; maternally inherited
- **Chloroplast DNA (cpDNA)** -- found in plant chloroplasts; evidence for endosymbiotic origin

## DNA Replication

Before a cell divides, it must copy its entire genome. Replication is **semi-conservative** -- each new DNA molecule contains one original strand and one newly synthesised strand.

### Steps of Replication

1. **Unwinding** -- *Helicase* enzymes separate the two strands; *topoisomerases* relieve the resulting tension
2. **Priming** -- *Primase* lays down short RNA primers to provide a starting point
3. **Elongation** -- *DNA polymerase* reads the template strand (3' to 5') and synthesises the new strand (5' to 3')
   - The **leading strand** is synthesised continuously
   - The **lagging strand** is synthesised in short fragments called *Okazaki fragments*
4. **Ligation** -- *DNA ligase* joins the Okazaki fragments into a continuous strand
5. **Proofreading** -- DNA polymerase has built-in error-checking, achieving an error rate of roughly 1 in 10 billion base pairs

## Gene Expression

The information in DNA is used to build proteins through two major steps:

### Transcription (DNA to RNA)

- **RNA polymerase** binds to a *promoter* region on the DNA
- It reads the template strand and synthesises a complementary **messenger RNA (mRNA)** molecule
- In mRNA, **uracil (U)** replaces thymine (T)
- In eukaryotes, the mRNA is processed (capping, splicing, polyadenylation) before export from the nucleus

### Translation (RNA to Protein)

- **Ribosomes** read the mRNA in three-nucleotide units called **codons**
- Each codon specifies one of the 20 standard amino acids (or a stop signal)
- **Transfer RNA (tRNA)** molecules deliver the appropriate amino acids
- The ribosome links amino acids into a polypeptide chain that folds into a functional protein

There are **64 possible codons** (4 bases x 4 x 4), encoding 20 amino acids plus 3 stop signals. This redundancy is called the *degeneracy* of the genetic code.

## Mutations and DNA Damage

### Types of Mutations

- **Point mutations** -- single base changes (substitutions)
- **Insertions and deletions** -- addition or removal of bases, potentially causing *frameshift* errors
- **Chromosomal rearrangements** -- large-scale inversions, translocations, or duplications

### Sources of DNA Damage

DNA is constantly under assault from:

- **Ultraviolet light** -- causes thymine dimers
- **Oxidative stress** -- produces base modifications and strand breaks (~150,000 oxidative lesions per cell at any time)
- **Chemical mutagens** -- alkylating agents, intercalating dyes, etc.

### Repair Mechanisms

Cells possess multiple repair pathways:

- *Base excision repair* -- fixes single damaged bases
- *Nucleotide excision repair* -- removes bulky lesions (e.g., thymine dimers)
- *Mismatch repair* -- corrects replication errors
- *Homologous recombination* and *non-homologous end joining* -- repair double-strand breaks

When repair fails, accumulated damage contributes to **aging** and **cancer**.

## Epigenetics

Beyond the base sequence itself, chemical modifications to DNA and histones regulate gene expression without altering the underlying code:

- **5-methylcytosine** -- the most common DNA modification in mammals; important for X-chromosome inactivation and genomic imprinting
- **5-hydroxymethylcytosine** -- particularly abundant in brain tissue
- **Histone acetylation/methylation** -- alters chromatin structure, making genes more or less accessible

These *epigenetic* marks can be inherited across cell divisions and, in some cases, across generations.

## Biotechnology Applications

### DNA Profiling (Forensics)

By analysing variable regions of DNA that differ between individuals, forensic scientists can create a **genetic fingerprint** with near-certainty of identification. Applications include criminal investigations, paternity testing, and disaster victim identification.

### Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR)

Invented by **Kary Mullis** in 1983, PCR amplifies tiny quantities of DNA through repeated cycles of heating and cooling. It is indispensable in:

- Medical diagnostics (e.g., COVID-19 testing)
- Forensic analysis
- Cloning and sequencing

### Genetic Engineering and CRISPR

Modern tools -- especially **CRISPR-Cas9** -- allow precise editing of DNA sequences. Applications include:

- Correcting disease-causing mutations (gene therapy)
- Engineering drought-resistant crops
- Creating animal models for research

### DNA Sequencing

Technologies ranging from Sanger sequencing to modern *nanopore* sequencing can read the complete genetic code of organisms. The **Human Genome Project** (completed in 2003) was a landmark achievement, and today a human genome can be sequenced in hours for under $1,000.

## Alternative DNA Structures

While the classic **B-form** double helix is the most common, DNA can adopt other conformations:

- **A-DNA** -- a wider, right-handed helix found under dehydrated conditions
- **Z-DNA** -- a left-handed helix that may play a role in gene regulation
- **G-quadruplexes** -- four-stranded structures formed by guanine-rich sequences (e.g., in telomeres)
- **Hachimoji DNA** -- a synthetic 8-base system demonstrating that life's 4-base code is not the only viable genetic alphabet
