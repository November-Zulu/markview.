# The Periodic Table

The periodic table is an ordered arrangement of the chemical elements into rows called **periods** and columns called **groups**. It is one of the most recognisable and useful tools in all of science, encoding the **periodic law** -- the principle that element properties recur in a predictable pattern when elements are arranged by atomic number.

## History

### Early Attempts

Before a comprehensive table emerged, several scientists noticed patterns among the elements:

- **Johann Dobereiner** (1829) grouped elements into *triads* with similar properties
- **John Newlands** (1865) proposed a *Law of Octaves*, noting that properties repeated every eight elements -- though the idea was initially ridiculed

### Mendeleev's Breakthrough

In **1869**, Russian chemist **Dmitri Mendeleev** published the first periodic table to achieve widespread acceptance. His key innovations:

1. He arranged elements by **atomic mass** and observed repeating chemical properties
2. He left **intentional gaps** where no known element fit the pattern
3. He used those gaps to *predict the properties of undiscovered elements*

> Mendeleev successfully predicted the existence and properties of elements later named **gallium** (discovered 1875), **scandium** (1879), and **germanium** (1886). These confirmations established the periodic table's credibility.

### Modern Refinements

- **Henry Moseley** (1913) demonstrated that elements should be ordered by **atomic number** (proton count), not atomic mass, resolving several anomalies in Mendeleev's arrangement
- **Glenn T. Seaborg** (1945) reconfigured the table to properly place the *f-block* elements (lanthanides and actinides), producing the modern layout we use today
- By **2016**, all 118 elements through oganesson had been confirmed, completing the first seven periods

## Structure

### Atomic Number

Each element has a unique **atomic number (Z)** equal to the number of protons in its nucleus. This number alone defines what element an atom is -- all carbon atoms have exactly 6 protons, all gold atoms have 79.

### Periods (Rows)

The table has **seven periods**. A new period begins each time an electron starts filling a new principal energy shell:

| Period | Elements | Electron Shell Being Filled |
|--------|----------|-----------------------------|
| 1 | H, He | 1s |
| 2 | Li -- Ne | 2s, 2p |
| 3 | Na -- Ar | 3s, 3p |
| 4 | K -- Kr | 4s, 3d, 4p |
| 5 | Rb -- Xe | 5s, 4d, 5p |
| 6 | Cs -- Rn | 6s, 4f, 5d, 6p |
| 7 | Fr -- Og | 7s, 5f, 6d, 7p |

### Groups (Columns)

Elements in the same group share the same **valence electron configuration** and therefore exhibit similar chemical behaviour.

## Major Element Groups

| Group | Name | Example Elements | Key Characteristics |
|-------|------|-----------------|-------------------|
| 1 | **Alkali metals** | Li, Na, K, Rb, Cs | Highly reactive; 1 valence electron; soft, silvery metals |
| 2 | **Alkaline earth metals** | Be, Mg, Ca, Sr, Ba | Moderately reactive; 2 valence electrons |
| 3--12 | **Transition metals** | Fe, Cu, Au, Ag, Pt | Variable oxidation states; often coloured compounds |
| 13 | **Triels** (Boron group) | B, Al, Ga, In | Mix of metalloid and metal character |
| 14 | **Tetrels** (Carbon group) | C, Si, Ge, Sn, Pb | Ranges from nonmetal (C) to metal (Pb) |
| 15 | **Pnictogens** | N, P, As, Sb, Bi | Essential for life (N, P); variable chemistry |
| 16 | **Chalcogens** | O, S, Se, Te | Oxygen is the most abundant element in Earth's crust |
| 17 | **Halogens** | F, Cl, Br, I | Highly reactive nonmetals; form salts with metals |
| 18 | **Noble gases** | He, Ne, Ar, Kr, Xe | Chemically inert; full valence shells |
| -- | **Lanthanides** (f-block) | La, Ce, Nd, Eu | Rare earth elements; similar chemistry within the series |
| -- | **Actinides** (f-block) | Ac, Th, U, Pu | Includes all naturally radioactive heavy elements |

## Periodic Trends

Several fundamental properties change *predictably* across the table:

- **Atomic radius** -- *increases* going down a group (more electron shells) and *decreases* going left to right across a period (greater nuclear charge pulls electrons inward)
- **Ionization energy** -- *increases* across a period and *decreases* down a group. Fluorine and noble gases have the highest values.
- **Electronegativity** -- *increases* toward the upper right corner. **Fluorine** is the most electronegative element; **francium** is the least.
- **Electron affinity** -- nonmetals (especially halogens) readily gain electrons; metals readily lose them
- **Metallic character** -- *increases* going down and to the left. The lower-left corner contains the most metallic elements (Cs, Fr).

## Notable Elements

- **Hydrogen** -- the simplest and most abundant element in the universe; does not fit neatly into any group
- **Carbon** -- the basis of organic chemistry and all known life
- **Iron** -- the most common element on Earth by mass; essential for haemoglobin
- **Uranium** -- the heaviest naturally abundant element; used in nuclear energy
- **Oganesson (Og, Z=118)** -- the most recently confirmed element; completes period 7

## The Future of the Periodic Table

All 118 confirmed elements fill the first seven rows. Research teams around the world are attempting to synthesise elements **119** and beyond, which would begin an eighth period. Theoretical calculations suggest these superheavy elements may not follow established periodic trends due to strong *relativistic effects* on inner electrons.

The periodic table continues to evolve with the progress of science -- a living document that has organised our understanding of matter for over 150 years.
