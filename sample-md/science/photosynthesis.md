# Photosynthesis

Photosynthesis is the biological process by which plants, algae, and cyanobacteria convert **light energy** into **chemical energy**, storing it in the bonds of carbohydrate molecules such as glucose and sucrose. It is the foundation of nearly all food chains on Earth and the primary source of atmospheric oxygen.

## The Chemical Equation

The overall reaction for oxygenic photosynthesis can be written as:

> **6 CO2 + 6 H2O + light energy --> C6H12O6 + 6 O2**

Carbon dioxide and water, energised by sunlight, yield sugar and oxygen. Some photosynthetic bacteria use hydrogen sulfide instead of water, releasing sulfur rather than oxygen.

## Where Photosynthesis Happens

### Chloroplast Structure

In plants and algae, photosynthesis takes place inside **chloroplasts** -- double-membraned organelles found mainly in leaf mesophyll cells. A single square millimetre of leaf tissue can contain between 450,000 and 800,000 chloroplasts.

Inside the chloroplast:

- **Thylakoid membranes** -- flattened sac-like structures stacked into *grana*; site of the light-dependent reactions
- **Stroma** -- the aqueous fluid surrounding the thylakoids; site of the Calvin cycle
- **Chloroplast DNA** -- a small circular genome inherited from ancient cyanobacterial ancestors (endosymbiosis)

### Photosynthetic Pigments

| Pigment | Colour Absorbed | Colour Reflected | Role |
|---------|----------------|-----------------|------|
| Chlorophyll *a* | Red, blue | Green | Primary reaction-centre pigment |
| Chlorophyll *b* | Red, blue-violet | Yellow-green | Accessory pigment |
| Carotenoids | Blue, green | Orange-yellow | Photoprotection and accessory light harvesting |
| Phycoerythrin | Green, blue | Red | Found in red algae; enables deep-water photosynthesis |

These pigments are organised into **antenna complexes** that funnel captured light energy toward the photosystem reaction centres.

## The Two Stages of Photosynthesis

### Stage 1: Light-Dependent Reactions

These reactions occur in the thylakoid membranes and convert light energy into the chemical energy carriers **ATP** and **NADPH**.

**The Z-scheme mechanism:**

1. **Photosystem II** absorbs a photon, exciting an electron.
2. The excited electron passes through an *electron transport chain*, pumping H+ ions across the thylakoid membrane.
3. **ATP synthase** uses the resulting proton gradient to produce ATP.
4. The electron enters **Photosystem I**, where a second photon boosts it to an even higher energy level.
5. The high-energy electron reduces NADP+ to **NADPH**.

**Water splitting (photolysis):**

To replace the electrons lost from Photosystem II, water molecules are split by an *oxygen-evolving complex* containing manganese and calcium ions:

> 2 H2O --> O2 + 4 H+ + 4 e-

This is the source of all the **oxygen** released by photosynthesis.

### Stage 2: The Calvin Cycle (Light-Independent Reactions)

Although called "light-independent," the Calvin cycle depends entirely on ATP and NADPH from the light reactions. It takes place in the stroma.

1. **Carbon fixation** -- The enzyme *RuBisCO* combines CO2 with ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate (RuBP), producing two molecules of 3-phosphoglycerate (3-PGA).
2. **Reduction** -- ATP and NADPH convert 3-PGA into glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate (G3P).
3. **Regeneration** -- Most G3P molecules are recycled to regenerate RuBP, sustaining the cycle.
4. The remaining G3P molecules are used to build glucose, sucrose, starch, and other organic compounds.

For every **three molecules of CO2** fixed, the cycle consumes 9 ATP and 6 NADPH.

## Adaptations: C3, C4, and CAM Plants

### C3 Photosynthesis

Over 90% of plant species use the standard Calvin cycle (C3 pathway). However, under hot, dry conditions, plants close their stomata to conserve water, causing CO2 levels inside the leaf to drop. RuBisCO then fixes O2 instead of CO2 -- a wasteful process called **photorespiration**.

### C4 Photosynthesis

About 3% of plant species (including *maize*, *sorghum*, and *sugarcane*) have evolved a workaround:

- CO2 is first captured by **PEP carboxylase** in mesophyll cells, forming a 4-carbon compound
- This compound is shuttled to **bundle sheath cells**, where CO2 is released at high concentration for RuBisCO
- Photorespiration is virtually eliminated

### CAM Photosynthesis

Desert plants such as *cacti* and *succulents* use **Crassulacean acid metabolism**:

- Stomata open at *night*, fixing CO2 into malic acid
- During the *day*, stomata close (saving water), and stored CO2 is released internally for the Calvin cycle

## Factors Affecting Photosynthetic Rate

Several environmental variables influence how fast photosynthesis proceeds:

- **Light intensity** -- Rate increases with light up to a *saturation point*, then plateaus
- **CO2 concentration** -- Higher CO2 generally increases the rate, especially for C3 plants
- **Temperature** -- Enzyme activity peaks in a species-specific optimum range (typically 25--35 degrees C)
- **Water availability** -- Drought causes stomatal closure, limiting CO2 intake

## Photosynthetic Efficiency

Plants typically convert light into chemical energy with an efficiency of **3--6%**. The rest is lost as heat, with a small fraction (1--2%) re-emitted as chlorophyll fluorescence.

In natural conditions, overall efficiency ranges from 0.1% to 8%, depending on species and environment. By comparison, commercial solar panels achieve 6--20% efficiency -- motivating ongoing research into improving crop photosynthesis through biotechnology.

## Evolutionary Significance

Photosynthesis is ancient. Fossils of filamentous photosynthetic organisms have been dated to approximately **3.4 billion years ago**. Early photosynthesisers likely used hydrogen sulfide rather than water as an electron donor.

The emergence of *oxygenic* photosynthesis in **cyanobacteria** around 2 billion years ago triggered the **Great Oxidation Event**, fundamentally transforming Earth's atmosphere and enabling the evolution of complex aerobic life.

> The average rate of energy captured by global photosynthesis is approximately **130 terawatts** -- roughly eight times current total human energy consumption.

Each year, photosynthetic organisms convert around **100--115 billion tonnes** of carbon into biomass, sustaining virtually all ecosystems on the planet.
