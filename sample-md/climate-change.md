# Climate Change

Climate change refers to the ongoing increase in global average temperature and its broader effects on Earth's climate system. The term became widely used after NASA scientist **James Hansen** referenced it in his 1988 congressional testimony. Scientists distinguish between *"global warming"* (temperature increase alone) and *"climate change"* (temperature plus wider climate effects).

## Primary Causes

### Greenhouse Gas Emissions

The atmosphere now contains roughly **50% more carbon dioxide** than pre-industrial levels. In 2019, human-caused emissions totalled 59 billion tonnes of CO2 equivalent:

- **75%** from carbon dioxide (CO2)
- **18%** from methane (CH4)
- **7%** from other gases (nitrous oxide, fluorinated gases)

### Fossil Fuel Combustion

Coal, oil, and natural gas burning since the Industrial Revolution is the primary driver of warming. These fuels power:

1. Transportation
2. Manufacturing
3. Heating
4. Electricity generation

### Deforestation

Forest destruction releases stored carbon and eliminates carbon sinks. Between 2001 and 2018, **27% of deforestation** enabled agricultural expansion, making land-use change a significant emissions source.

### Industrial Processes

Cement, steel, and fertiliser production release additional CO2 through chemical reactions independent of energy use.

## Evidence of Climate Change

### Temperature Records

> Earth's surface has warmed nearly **1.5 degrees C** since industrialisation. The 2014-2023 decade averaged 1.19 degrees C above the pre-industrial baseline (1850-1900).

### Ocean Heating

Oceans absorb over **90%** of the warming's heat energy, with measurable increases in ocean heat content over recent decades. This absorption has slowed atmospheric warming but comes with severe consequences for marine ecosystems.

### Sea Level Rise

Sea levels are rising at **4.8 cm per decade** (2014-2023) due to:

- Thermal expansion of warming ocean water
- Melting of glaciers and ice sheets
- Loss of ice mass from Greenland and Antarctica

### Arctic Changes

Northern regions warm between **three and four times faster** than the global average, with significant sea ice decline and permafrost thawing releasing additional greenhouse gases.

### Historical Proxy Data

Ice cores, tree rings, and coral records show current warming is *unprecedented for several thousand years*, providing critical context beyond the instrumental record.

## Observed Environmental Effects

### Extreme Weather

- **Heat waves and wildfires** intensify in frequency and severity
- **Monsoon precipitation** has increased since 1980
- **Tropical cyclone intensity** likely increases, though frequency has not changed

### Ecosystem Shifts

Species are migrating poleward at approximately **1.5 km per year** (documented in North American birds over the past 55 years). Coral bleaching accelerates from marine heatwaves, threatening reef ecosystems worldwide.

### Biodiversity Loss

- Ocean acidification hampers shell-forming organisms
- Harmful algal blooms increase in frequency and extent
- Climate change threatens extinction for many species across terrestrial and marine environments

### Polar Ice Loss

Arctic sea ice continues shrinking. The Greenland and West Antarctic ice sheets show accelerating melting, contributing to sea level rise and altering ocean circulation patterns.

## Tipping Points and Irreversible Changes

Certain thresholds, once crossed, trigger changes that cannot be reversed on human timescales:

| Tipping Point | Threshold | Consequence |
|--------------|-----------|-------------|
| Greenland Ice Sheet | 1.7-2.3 degrees C warming | Unstoppable melting, metres of sea level rise |
| Atlantic Ocean Circulation | Could occur within decades | Disrupted weather patterns across Europe |
| Coral Reef Ecosystems | Ongoing thermal stress | Worldwide population collapse |
| Amazon Rainforest | Cumulative climate stress | Irreversible transition to savanna |

> Long-term ocean impacts persist for **centuries to millennia** due to CO2's atmospheric longevity, with an estimated 2.3 metres per degree Celsius of total sea level rise after 2,000 years.

## Future Projections

The World Meteorological Organization estimates a nearly **50% probability** that the 2024-2028 five-year average will exceed +1.5 degrees C. The IPCC expects the 20-year average to exceed this threshold in the early 2030s.

### IPCC Scenarios for 2100

1. **Very low emissions**: 1.0-1.8 degrees C warming
2. **Intermediate emissions**: 2.1-3.5 degrees C warming
3. **Very high emissions**: 3.3-5.7 degrees C warming

Under current Paris Agreement pledges, warming would reach approximately **2.8 degrees C** by century's end.

## Impacts on Human Systems

### Agriculture and Food Security

Crop yields face stress from heat, drought, and shifting precipitation patterns. Food security deteriorates most severely in vulnerable regions that are least equipped to adapt.

### Human Health

The WHO identifies climate change as **"one of the biggest threats to global health in the 21st century."** Impacts include:

- Heat-related illness and mortality
- Expansion of vector-borne diseases (malaria, dengue)
- Malnutrition from agricultural disruption
- Mental health effects from displacement and disaster

### Economic Consequences

Economic losses mount from infrastructure damage, agricultural disruption, and adaptation costs. *Poorer communities bear disproportionate burdens* despite contributing minimal emissions.

### Migration and Conflict

Environmental degradation forces human displacement, creating climate refugees and potential resource-based conflicts in affected regions.

### Water Resources

Droughts intensify in some regions while floods increase in others, stressing freshwater supplies that billions of people depend upon.

## Mitigation Strategies

### Renewable Energy Transition

Wind, solar, hydroelectric, and nuclear power can replace fossil fuels for electricity generation and enable transportation electrification. Renewable energy costs have fallen dramatically:

- Solar photovoltaic costs dropped over **85%** between 2010 and 2020
- Wind energy costs fell by approximately **55%** in the same period

### Energy Conservation

Building efficiency improvements, industrial process optimisation, and reduced consumption lower overall energy demand.

### Carbon Dioxide Removal

Strategies include:

- **Reforestation** -- increasing forest cover to absorb CO2
- **Soil carbon sequestration** -- farming methods that store carbon in soil
- **Direct air capture** -- emerging technologies that extract CO2 directly from the atmosphere

### Industrial Transformation

Cleaner manufacturing processes for cement, steel, and chemicals can reduce process emissions. Green hydrogen and electrification offer pathways for hard-to-abate sectors.

### Fossil Fuel Phase-Out

Ending subsidies and implementing regulations to support transition away from coal, oil, and gas are essential steps toward decarbonisation.

## Adaptation Measures

Communities worldwide are implementing adaptation strategies:

- Flood control infrastructure and coastal defences
- Drought-resistant crop varieties
- Early warning systems for extreme weather
- Urban heat island mitigation through green infrastructure

However, some adaptation limits have been reached, particularly for island nations facing existential sea level rise and Arctic regions experiencing rapid, irreversible warming.

## International Climate Agreements

### The Paris Agreement (2015)

Nations collectively agreed to keep warming **"well under 2 degrees C"** above pre-industrial levels, with efforts to limit it to 1.5 degrees C. Countries submit *nationally determined contributions* (NDCs) outlining their emissions reduction plans.

### Earlier Frameworks

- **Kyoto Protocol** (1997) -- First binding international emissions reduction treaty
- **Copenhagen Accord** (2009) -- Recognised the scientific view for limiting warming to 2 degrees C

### Supporting Institutions

- The **IPCC** provides scientific assessments guiding policy
- **UNEP** tracks progress toward net-zero emissions targets
- Most countries now aim to achieve **net-zero emissions**, though implementation varies significantly by development level and economic structure

## Scientific Consensus

The scientific community **overwhelmingly agrees** that present warming results from human activities. Multiple independent lines of evidence corroborate this conclusion:

- Temperature records from thousands of weather stations
- Ice core data spanning hundreds of thousands of years
- Ocean heat content measurements
- Satellite observations of sea level and ice extent
- Atmospheric composition analysis

Human fingerprints distinguish anthropogenic warming from natural cycles. Solar forcing is ruled out because only the lower atmosphere has warmed; volcanic activity explains negligible recent warming trends.

> Climate change represents an unprecedented environmental crisis requiring immediate, comprehensive action across energy systems, land use, and industrial processes to prevent irreversible ecological and social consequences.
