# Open-Source Software

Open-source software (OSS) is computer software released under licences granting users rights to **use, study, change, and distribute** the software and its source code. This model enables collaborative development where any capable user can participate online, fostering public trust through code transparency.

## Definition and Philosophy

The [Open Source Initiative](https://opensource.org/) (OSI) provides the internationally recognised definition of open-source software. However, important distinctions exist between *"open source"* and *"free software"* -- terms that Richard Stallman argues describe "almost the same category of software" but carry different philosophical implications.

> The free software movement emphasises social freedom ideals, while the open-source movement prioritises practical development benefits and business pragmatism.

## Historical Evolution

### Early Computing Era (1950s-1970s)

Programmers historically shared software freely to advance the field. Early systems like **Unix** provided source code access, enabling widespread collaboration. This culture of open sharing declined with the commercial software industry's rise in the 1970s and 1980s.

### The Free Software Movement (1984-1991)

**Richard Stallman**, frustrated by proprietary restrictions at MIT, launched the free software movement in 1984:

1. Created **GNU** -- a Unix-compatible operating system
2. Founded the **Free Software Foundation** (FSF) in 1985 to promote software freedom
3. Introduced the revolutionary concept of *copyleft* licensing
4. Released the **GNU General Public License** (GPL) in 1989, updated in 1991

In 1991, **Linus Torvalds** introduced the Linux kernel, which combined with GNU to create what became the Linux operating system -- a watershed moment for open-source adoption.

### The Open Source Initiative (1998)

As commercial interest grew, **Christine Peterson** suggested "open source" as a rebranding term in 1998. This terminology emphasised business pragmatism over ideological stance, leading to the creation of the OSI and the establishment of widely accepted definitional standards.

## Licensing Models

Two primary licensing approaches dominate the open-source landscape:

### Permissive Licences

Licences like **BSD**, **MIT**, and **Apache** allow recipients to implement copyright rights without requiring identical licensing for derivative works. This enables broad adoption and commercial integration.

### Copyleft Licences

Licences like **GPL**, **MPL**, and **EPL** require recipients to use matching licences for distributions:

- **Strong copyleft** mandates identical licensing for all derivatives
- **Weak copyleft** applies conditions selectively

> The 2008 *Jacobson v. Katzer* case established legal enforcement precedent, cementing copyright law protection for open-source licence terms.

## Key Projects and Their Impact

| Project | Year | Significance |
|---------|------|-------------|
| Unix | 1960s | Pioneered collaborative development ideals |
| BSD | 1978-1995 | Demonstrated free public code sharing |
| GNU/Linux | 1984-1991 | Archetypal open-source operating system |
| Apache | 1995 | Became the dominant web server |

### Unix

Unix pioneered collaborative development ideals before commercialisation, establishing decentralised collaboration patterns and community distrust of proprietary models.

### Berkeley Software Distribution

**BSD** (1978-1995) demonstrated free public code sharing, creating non-Unix variants when Unix became commercialised.

### GNU/Linux

Combined Stallman's ideological vision with Torvalds' practical kernel implementation, becoming the most widely known open-source operating system.

### Apache

Emerged from frustration with proprietary web servers, Apache became the *dominant* web server and established alternative licensing approaches within FOSS communities.

## Development Methodology

Eric S. Raymond's influential 1997 essay **"The Cathedral and the Bazaar"** contrasted two models:

- **The Cathedral**: Traditional hierarchical development -- centralised, planned, and controlled
- **The Bazaar**: Open-source's distributed approach -- decentralised, organic, and community-driven

OSS development emphasises:

- Treating users as **co-developers** with source access
- **Early releases** enabling community participation
- **Frequent code integration** preventing bug accumulation
- **Modular architecture** supporting parallel development
- **Dynamic decision-making** responsive to evolving requirements

> This approach leverages "Linus's law," which posits that *given enough eyeballs, all bugs are shallow*.

## Business Models and Industry Adoption

### Economic Value

A 2024 estimate values OSS to firms at **$8.8 trillion** -- approximately 3.5 times current spending without open-source reliance. Over 90% of companies incorporate OSS components into proprietary products.

### Service-Based Models

Companies increasingly adopt service-based business models:

1. Controlling OSS products while charging for improvements and integration
2. Offering enterprise support and consulting
3. Building **Software-as-a-Service** (SaaS) offerings on OSS components

### Corporate Involvement

Major technology companies -- historically sceptical -- now maintain official open-source presences. *Microsoft*, once opposing OSS as an "intellectual property destroyer" (2001), has since become one of its largest contributors. IBM, Oracle, and many others represent significant corporate stakeholders.

## Governance and Policy

Governments increasingly recognise OSS value:

- **UK** (2004/2009) -- Committed to considering open-source solutions alongside proprietary alternatives
- **Germany** -- Established the Sovereign Tech Fund supporting critical infrastructure maintenance
- **France** -- Policies incentivising OSS increased contributions to nearly 600,000 annually, correlating with 18% tech startup growth and 14% IT employment increases

## Demographics and Representation

Contributions cluster geographically near technology hubs. The 2021 leaders by total contributions were:

1. United States
2. China
3. Germany
4. India
5. United Kingdom

Per-capita contributors concentrate in Iceland, Switzerland, Norway, Sweden, and Finland.

### Gender Disparities

Gender representation remains critically low -- only **1.5%** of developers in 2002, rising to **9.8%** by 2021. Women face persistent biases including longer feedback times, heightened code scrutiny, lower acceptance rates, and hostile community interactions.

## The Future of Open Source

OSS success remains contested. While **Android** and **Ubuntu** represent mainstream achievements, critics cite concerning compromises:

- Android's downplayed OSS emphasis
- Convenience prioritised over freedom in Ubuntu
- Invasive user tracking in ostensibly "open" platforms

The community must clarify what OSS represents and how to protect its fundamental principles amid mainstream adoption. The open-collaboration principles underlying OSS continue to extend to **open content**, **open hardware**, and broader movements for transparency in technology.
