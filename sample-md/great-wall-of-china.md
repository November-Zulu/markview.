# The Great Wall of China

The Great Wall of China is a **series of fortifications** built across the historical northern
borders of China to protect against nomadic groups from the Eurasian Steppe. Beyond military
defense, the structure served multiple functions including border control, immigration
regulation, and trade management along the Silk Road.

> The Great Wall represents "one of the most impressive architectural feats in history."

## Historical Development

### Early Construction

The Chinese mastered wall-building techniques during the **Spring and Autumn period**
(8th--5th centuries BC). Various warring states constructed fortifications using stone or
rammed earth packed between wooden frames.

### Dynastic Contributions

1. **Qin Dynasty (221 BC)** --- The First Emperor, *Qin Shi Huang*, ordered destruction of
   internal walls dividing his unified empire, then constructed new fortifications connecting
   existing structures along northern borders to counter the Xiongnu threat.

2. **Han Dynasty** --- Extended the wall considerably westward. Historical records indicate
   it was "the longest of all walls, from Mamitu, near Yumenguan, to Liaodong."

3. **Ming Dynasty (1368--1644)** --- Made the most substantial contributions, following
   defeats by the Oirats. This period introduced **stronger construction** using bricks and
   stone rather than rammed earth, with estimates of up to *25,000 watchtowers* built.

## Physical Dimensions

The collective fortifications span an extraordinary distance:

| Measurement          | Distance                  |
|----------------------|---------------------------|
| Total length         | 21,196.18 km (13,170 mi)  |
| Ming Wall alone      | ~8,850 km                 |
| Components           | Wall sections, trenches, natural barriers |

The wall varies in height and width depending on the section and era of construction. The
**Jinshanling** section, for instance, ranges from 5 to 8 meters in height and features
67 watchtowers.

## Construction Materials and Methods

The materials used evolved significantly over the centuries:

- **Earlier walls** --- rammed earth, stone, and wood
- **Ming Dynasty walls** --- bricks, tiles, lime, and rectangular stone blocks for
  foundations and gateways
- **Mortar** --- a unique *sticky rice mortar*, consisting of sticky rice soup mixed with
  slaked lime, was extensively used to hold bricks together

## Notable Sections

### Badaling

The **most famous stretch** near Beijing, Badaling attracted nearly **10 million visitors in
2018** before authorities implemented daily visitor caps of 65,000.

### Jinshanling

Features dramatic steep mountain terrain with stunning views. This section is popular with
hikers and photographers seeking a less crowded experience.

### Shanhai Pass

Considered the traditional **eastern terminus** of the Great Wall, marked by the *"Old Dragon
Head"* --- the point where the wall meets the Bohai Sea.

## Current Condition

The wall faces significant deterioration. A 2012 report revealed sobering statistics:

- **22%** of the Ming Great Wall --- or 1,961 km --- has **disappeared**
- Western sections constructed from mud are particularly vulnerable to erosion
- Sections near Beijing and tourist centers have been preserved or extensively renovated
- Remote areas remain in serious disrepair

## UNESCO and World Recognition

The Great Wall was designated a **UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1987** and was voted one of
the **New Seven Wonders of the World** in 2007.

### The Moon Myth

> The popular myth claiming the Great Wall is visible from the Moon is thoroughly debunked ---
> the wall's apparent width from that distance would equal "that of a human hair viewed from
> 3 km away."

However, the wall *may* be barely visible from low Earth orbit under ideal conditions, though
even this has been disputed by astronauts.

## Military Function and Garrison Life

The Great Wall was not merely a passive barrier. It functioned as a **complex military system**
with integrated components:

- **Watchtowers** --- used for surveillance and signal transmission via smoke or fire
- **Garrison stations** --- housed soldiers responsible for defending specific wall sections
- **Beacon towers** --- enabled rapid long-distance communication; a message could travel
  hundreds of kilometers in hours through a relay chain of fire signals
- **Gates and passes** --- controlled movement of people and goods, serving as customs
  checkpoints along trade routes

Soldiers stationed along the wall endured harsh conditions, particularly in the western
desert sections. Many walls were built using *conscripted labor*, and historical accounts
describe significant loss of life during construction --- a fact reflected in Chinese folklore
and the famous legend of **Meng Jiangnu**, whose tears supposedly collapsed a section of the
wall after her husband died during its construction.

## Tourism and Preservation

Today, the Great Wall is China's most iconic tourist attraction and a powerful symbol of
national identity. The Chinese government has enacted preservation laws, though enforcement
remains inconsistent across the wall's vast extent. Restoration efforts focus primarily on
sections near major cities, while organizations like the **China Great Wall Society** advocate
for broader conservation.
