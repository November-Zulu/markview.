# Photography

Photography is the art, application, and practice of creating images by recording light -- either through electronic sensors or light-sensitive chemical materials. The term derives from Greek roots: *photos* (light) and *graphe* (drawing), meaning **"drawing with light."**

## Early Foundations

The technology evolved from several precursor discoveries, most notably the **camera obscura** ("dark chamber"), documented since ancient times. Arab physicist *Ibn al-Haytham* (965-1040) provided the first correct analysis of the camera obscura and understood the relationship between focal points and pinholes, laying the groundwork for centuries of optical development.

## The Invention Era

### First Photographs

- **Nicephore Niepce** created the first permanent photoetch in 1822
- His *View from the Window at Le Gras* (1826) is the **earliest surviving photograph from nature**
- **Louis Daguerre** developed the daguerreotype process, announced publicly in 1839, reducing exposure time from hours to minutes

### The Negative-Positive Process

**William Fox Talbot** pioneered an alternative approach using paper-based negatives, creating the *calotype* process in 1840. Unlike daguerreotypes, Talbot's method could be used to print multiple positive copies -- establishing principles still fundamental to photography.

## Colour Photography

Early colour experiments faced significant technical barriers:

1. **1861** -- James Clerk Maxwell demonstrated the three-colour separation method
2. **1907** -- The *Autochrome* process became the first commercially successful colour system
3. **1935** -- **Kodachrome** introduced multi-layer emulsion technology, becoming the gold standard for decades

## Film Photography

**George Eastman** introduced flexible roll film in 1885, making photography accessible to amateurs. His company, Kodak, popularised the snapshot with the famous slogan:

> "You press the button, we do the rest."

Roll film enabled smaller, more portable cameras and democratised image-making beyond professional studios.

## The Digital Revolution

The shift from film to digital transformed photography entirely:

- **1981** -- Sony's *Mavica* camera pioneered electronic image capture
- **1991** -- Kodak unveiled the first commercial digital SLR camera
- **2000s** -- Digital cameras surpassed film in consumer adoption

Today, **more than 99% of photographs** taken worldwide use digital cameras or smartphone sensors.

## Types and Genres

Photography encompasses diverse specialisations:

- **Portrait and wedding** -- direct client work capturing people and events
- **Landscape** -- natural or human-made environments
- **Commercial** -- advertising, food, architectural, and product imagery
- **Photojournalism** -- documentary coverage of news events
- **Fine art** -- creative expression within gallery contexts
- **Wildlife** -- documenting animal behaviour in natural habitats
- **Street** -- candid scenes of everyday public life
- **Macro** -- extreme close-up images revealing fine detail

## Key Techniques

Photographers control several fundamental variables:

| Variable | Purpose |
|----------|---------|
| **Exposure** | Controlling the amount of light reaching the sensor or film |
| **Composition** | Arranging foreground, subject, and background layers |
| **Focal length** | Manipulating perspective and depth of field |
| **Lighting** | Strategic illumination of subjects |

Modern developments include **light field photography**, which allows focus adjustment after capture, and **full-spectrum photography** using ultraviolet and infrared wavelengths for scientific and artistic applications.

## Modern Photography

The 21st century has transformed photography through smartphone integration. Contemporary devices feature automatic colour management, autofocus, face detection, and image stabilisation -- *democratising high-quality image capture* for billions of people worldwide. Social media platforms like Instagram have made photographic and video recording pervasive in everyday life.

## Notable Pioneers

Photographers like **Alfred Stieglitz** and **Edward Weston** elevated the medium to fine art status, moving beyond imitation of painting styles toward what Weston called **"straight photography"** -- sharp, authentic imagery valued for its own aesthetic properties.
